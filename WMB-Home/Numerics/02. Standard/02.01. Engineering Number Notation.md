# Scientific Notation
Wimby uses [standard scientific notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_notation) of the form:

$$
m \times 10^n
$$

 ‚Äî where necessary for very large numbers and very small numbers.

> [!However...]
>  ¬∑ When clarity benefits the reader, Wimby may write out very large or very small numbers instead of using scientific notation.
>  ¬∑ In some contexts, $0.000000314$ communicates magnitude more intuitively than $3.14 \times 10^{-7}$.

## Engineering Notation
Engineering notation is a practical way of writing numbers so they stay readable and scale cleanly with SI prefixes.

It differs from scientific notation in one key way:  **the exponent is always a multiple of three**; e.g.:

$$
\begin{aligned}
&\alpha = 1.496 \times 10^6 \text{ km ‚Äî the distance from the Earth to the Sun} \\
&M_\odot = 3.33 \times 10^3 \text{ ‚Äî the mass of the Sun in terran masses} \\
&M_‚ôÉ = 955.1 \times 10^{-6} \text{ ‚Äî the mass of Jupiter in solar masses}
\end{aligned}
$$


This means every value can be expressed as a number from 1 to 999, times a familiar SI prefix.

This does two things **Wimby** cares deeply about:
- Humans can read it without a calculator.
- The prefix tells you, instantly, the scale you‚Äôre working at.
- Each prefix is $1000\times = 10^3$ larger than the next one below it, so:
	- $1$ millimeter = $1,000$ micrometers
	- $1$ micrometer = $1,000$ nanometers
	- $1$ kilometer = $1,000$ meters
	- $1$ megameter = $1,000$ kilometers

For context, here are the vernacular meanings of the most common prefixes:
- nano ‚ü∂ $1$ *billionth* = $^1/_{1000000000} = 0.000000001 = 1.0 \times 10^{-9}$
- micro ‚ü∂ $1$ *millionth* = $^1/_{1000000} = 0.000001 = 1.0 \times 10^{-6}$
- milli ‚ü∂ $1$ *thousandth* = $^1/_{1000} = 0.001 = 1.0 \times 10^{-3}$
- centi ‚ü∂ $1$ *hundredth* = $^1/_{100}  = 0.01 = 1.0 \times 10^{-2}$
- deci ‚ü∂ $1$ *tenth* = $^1/_{10} = 0.1 = 1.0 \times 10^{-1}$
- kilo ‚ü∂ $1$ *thousand* = $1,000 = 1.0 \times 10^{3}$
- mega ‚ü∂ $1$ *million* = $1,000,000 = 1.0 \times 10^{6}$
- giga ‚ü∂ $1$ *billion* = $1,000,000,000 = 1.0 \times 10^{9}$
- tera ‚ü∂ $1$ *trillion* = $1,000,000,000,000 = 1.0 \times 10^{12}$
## Why Engineering Notation Exists

Straight scientific notation is great for precision:
- $42,300 = 4.23 \times 10^5$    
- $0.00000000791 = 7.91 \times 10^{-9}$

‚Ä¶but scientific notation _still_ leaves you mentally converting:

- ‚ÄúIs that thousands? Millions? Nano-something?‚Äù

Engineering notation fixes that by snapping everything to the SI prefix ladder:

- $4.23 \times 10^3 \longrightarrow 423\; \text{k}$ (kilo)
- $4.23 \times 10^{-9} \longrightarrow 4.23\; \text{n}$ (nano)
- $0.000423 \longrightarrow 423\;\mu$ (micro)
    
Same digits ‚Äî **far more intuitive**.

## The Ratchet Rule

This is the heart of engineering notation:

> **Ratchet up the prefix when the number reaches $1000$.  
> Ratchet down the prefix when it drops below $1$.**

Examples (using various base units):
- $0.23\; Œº\text{L} \longrightarrow 230 \text{ nL}$ ($^{23}/_{100}$ microliters ‚ü∂ $230$ nanoliters)
- $12400 \text{ m} \longrightarrow 12.4 \text{ km}$ (meters ‚ü∂ kilometers)
-  $5,900,000\; J \longrightarrow 5.9 \text{ MJ}$ (joules ‚ü∂ megajoules)
- $0.0042 \text { t} \longrightarrow 4.2 \text{ mt}$ ($^{42}/_{1000}$ terrans ‚ü∂ $4.2$ milliterrans)
    
If the number is ever ‚â• 1000 or < 1,  just shift one prefix up or down.

This keeps values in the **1‚Äì999** window where human brains work best.
